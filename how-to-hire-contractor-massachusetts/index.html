MASSACHUSETTS HOMEOWNER GUIDE

How to Hire a Contractor
in Massachusetts

7 things to verify before you sign anything. A Massachusetts-specific checklist from a licensed Worcester County general contractor.

Massachusetts has some of the strongest homeowner protection laws in the country — but only if you know how to use them. Every year, Worcester County homeowners lose money to unlicensed contractors, vague contracts, and projects that go sideways without a permit. This guide covers exactly what to check before handing anyone a deposit.

This is written by Maverick City Builders, a licensed general contractor in Lancaster, MA serving Worcester County. We're not going to soft-pedal this — there are bad actors in this industry, and you need to know how to spot them.

THE 7-POINT CHECKLIST

STEP 1

Verify the HIC License

Any contractor doing home improvement work over $1,000 on a residential property in Massachusetts is required to hold a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) license. No exceptions.

Check it at: mass.gov → Office of Consumer Affairs → License Lookup. Search by name or license number. Make sure the status is "Active" and the expiration date is current.

STEP 2

Verify the CSL License

For structural work — anything involving load-bearing walls, foundations, framing, or major renovations — the contractor needs a Construction Supervisor License (CSL). This is separate from the HIC and requires passing a state exam.

Same lookup tool. Same "Active" status check. If they're doing structural work without a CSL, they're operating illegally and your homeowner's insurance may not cover damage from that work.

STEP 3

Confirm Insurance — Both Policies

General Liability — covers property damage. If a worker drops a beam through your floor, this pays for it. Minimum $1M coverage.

Ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) naming you as additional insured. Workers Compensation — covers injuries on your property. Without it, an injured worker can sue you directly. Do not skip this check.

STEP 4

Get a Written Contract — Massachusetts Law Requires It

Massachusetts law requires any home improvement contract over $1,000 to be in writing. The contract must include: contractor name and HIC number, full description of work, materials list, start and completion dates, total price, payment schedule, and a 3-day right of rescission notice.

If a contractor resists putting things in writing, walk away. A contract protects both parties. Any contractor who's done this before won't hesitate to sign one.

STEP 5

Confirm Who Pulls the Permits

A licensed contractor pulls permits in their name. If they ask you to pull permits, or suggest skipping permits to "save money," that's a major red flag. They either aren't licensed, aren't insured for permitted work, or are trying to avoid accountability.

Unpermitted work creates problems at resale (buyers' inspectors find it), voids homeowner's insurance on affected systems, and may require costly demo and redo if discovered by a building inspector.

STEP 6

Call Two References From the Past Year

Ask for two references from completed projects in the past 12 months — in Worcester County if possible. Call them. Ask: Did they finish on time? Did the final cost match the estimate? How did they handle problems when they came up? Would you hire them again?

A contractor who can't provide references, or whose references are all from 3+ years ago, is a contractor who hasn't had recent satisfied customers.

STEP 7

Review the Payment Schedule

Massachusetts law caps the initial deposit on home improvement contracts at one-third of the total contract price. Never pay more than 30% upfront. Payments should be tied to project milestones — not arbitrary dates or requests.

If a contractor asks for 50%+ upfront or wants cash only, walk away. Legitimate contractors have supplier accounts and cash flow — they don't need your money to buy materials before starting.

5 RED FLAGS TO WALK AWAY FROM

  • Can't provide HIC or CSL license number on request
  • Asks you to pull the permits instead of them
  • Wants more than 33% deposit upfront, or cash only
  • No written contract, or resists putting scope in writing
  • Can't name a single reference from the past 12 months
