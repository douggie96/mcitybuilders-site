ABOUT CONCORD — WHAT THE HOUSING STOCK LOOKS LIKE

Concord is a Middlesex County town of about 20,000 known for its role in American history and its position as one of the more desirable residential communities in the greater Boston market. The combination of strong school system reputation, historic character, open space preservation, and proximity to Routes 2 and 128 makes Concord a high-demand market where home values significantly exceed the Worcester County median.

The housing stock in Concord is more varied than most towns we serve. Concord Center — the historic core near Monument Square and the Battle Road — has a mix of 18th- and 19th-century Colonials, Victorians, and Federal-style homes, many of them on the National Register of Historic Places or subject to local historic district review. West Concord along Commonwealth Avenue and Sudbury Road has a denser mix of 1940s–1970s capes, ranches, and colonials — the highest volume of remodeling work we do in town comes from this district. Nine Acre Corner in the southeastern part of town, near the Sudbury border, has a mix of 1960s–1980s colonials and contemporaries on larger lots.

The most common remodeling scope in Concord depends on the neighborhood. In West Concord and Nine Acre Corner, the projects closely resemble what we do in Acton and Stow — kitchen gut renovations in mid-century layouts, bathroom updates, and deck additions. Near Concord Center, we work more carefully around existing historic fabric: matching existing trim profiles, using appropriate material selections, and in some cases coordinating with the Concord Historic Districts Commission on exterior work. MCB has the licensing and experience to navigate both contexts.

Permits are filed with the Concord Building Division at Town House on Monument Square. Concord's permit review is thorough — standard residential permits typically take 2–3 weeks. Work affecting historically significant structures may require additional Historic Districts Commission review. MCB handles all permit applications and commission coordination as part of our project management role — Concord homeowners do not need to navigate the process themselves.

Do historic district rules affect remodeling in Concord MA?

Yes, for properties within Concord's designated historic districts — primarily near Concord Center and the Battle Road corridor. Exterior changes to structures in the historic district require Concord Historic Districts Commission review. MCB coordinates this process as part of our project management. Interior work in most Concord homes is not subject to historic district review. We identify historic district status during the estimate walk-through.

How do permits work for remodeling in Concord MA?

Permits are filed with the Concord Building Division at Town House on Monument Square. Standard residential permits typically take 2–3 weeks to approve — Concord's review is thorough. MCB handles all permit applications, coordinates any Historic Districts Commission review required, and schedules all inspections. Concord homeowners do not need to visit the building department or track permits themselves.

What are the most common remodeling projects in Concord homes?

In West Concord and Nine Acre Corner, the most common projects are full kitchen gut renovations in mid-century capes and colonials, primary bath additions in homes with only one and a half baths, and tub-to-shower conversions. Near Concord Center, historic restoration work — window replacement with appropriate profiles, trim repair, and kitchen updates that respect original millwork — is more common. Deck additions are popular on Concord's larger lots in all neighborhoods.
