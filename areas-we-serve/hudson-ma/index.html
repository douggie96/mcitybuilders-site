HUDSON MA — HOUSING STOCK AND REMODELING CONTEXT

Hudson sits at the eastern edge of Worcester County, bordered by Marlborough, Stow, Bolton, and Berlin, with a population of about 20,000. The town's identity was built on the Assabet River mill economy — shoe manufacturing and textiles drove Hudson's growth from the 1860s through the mid-20th century, and that history shows in the housing stock. Blocks near downtown along Main Street, Prospect Street, and South Street hold turn-of-the-century colonials and two-family homes that were built to last but have never seen a significant interior update. Meanwhile, the neighborhoods radiating out along Broad Street, Manning Street, and Cox Street were developed in the 1950s through 1970s with capes and ranches that are now in prime remodeling territory — well-built homes where original kitchens and baths have simply aged past their useful life.

Hudson's downtown revival adds a layer of activity we see reflected in homeowner demand. The rotary area and the Main Street corridor have seen significant commercial reinvestment over the past decade — restaurants, retail, and the pedestrian improvements that come with a town that's drawing younger buyers. Those buyers are purchasing the 1900s–1930s colonials near downtown and immediately planning kitchen and bathroom renovations. These older homes come with their own set of considerations: original lath-and-plaster walls that need careful demo, knob-and-tube wiring that must be evaluated before adding new circuits, and cast iron or galvanized supply lines that often get replaced during bathroom gut work when they're already exposed. We assess all of this during the estimate walk-through and price accordingly — no surprises after demo begins.

The 1960s ranches along Brigham Hill Road and the Broad Street corridor are a different profile. These homes typically have compact kitchens with original oak or painted wood cabinets, builder-grade laminate countertops, and 4-inch backsplash tile that dates the space immediately. Bathroom floors are usually original 1-inch hex tile over a mortar bed — dense and durable, but styled for a different era. The most common scope in this housing type is a full gut: demo to studs, new waterproofing on bathroom floors and shower walls, new tile, new vanity, new fixtures, and updated lighting. In the kitchen, the most frequent request is opening the wall to the adjacent dining room to create an open-concept layout — these ranches have the footprint for it and the improvement is dramatic.

The Hudson Building Department at Town Hall on Main Street handles all residential building permits. Projects requiring a permit include any kitchen remodel involving plumbing or electrical work, any bathroom renovation with new rough plumbing, and any deck attached to the house or over 200 square feet. Most residential permits in Hudson run 1 to 2 weeks from submission to approval. Maverick City Builders is based 12 miles away in Lancaster — we pull Hudson permits routinely and coordinate directly with the building department on all required inspections. Sterling, Bolton, and Berlin homeowners adjacent to Hudson also contact us regularly for comparable work.

RECENT PROJECTS IN HUDSON MA

Colonial kitchen gut renovation — downtown Hudson

1920s colonial near Prospect Street. The original kitchen had never been touched — tongue-and-groove plank walls, a cast iron sink, and a layout that predated modern appliance sizing. We demoed to studs, replaced galvanized supply lines, updated the electrical panel to 200-amp service, and installed semi-custom shaker cabinets in two-tone white and charcoal. Quartz countertops, a 36-inch farmhouse sink, subway tile backsplash, and LVP flooring that matched the existing hardwood in the adjacent dining room. Hudson Building Department permit pulled and all inspections closed. Six-week project from demo to final walkthrough.

Ranch hall bath refresh — Broad Street neighborhood

1968 ranch with original blue tile and a fiberglass tub surround that was failing at the seams. Full gut to studs: Schluter waterproofing system on the floor and shower walls, 12x24 large-format porcelain tile in a light grey, 60-inch tub-to-shower conversion with a frameless glass door, new vanity with an integrated undermount sink, updated lighting, and exhaust fan. Hudson Building Department permit for the plumbing work, rough and final inspections passed. Completed in 18 working days. The homeowner had been planning this project for three years and was surprised by how quickly it moved once permits were in hand.
